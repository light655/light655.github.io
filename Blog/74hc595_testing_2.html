Feb 28 2026 How much current can the 74HC595 shift register provide?

The 74HC595 is one of the most common logic ICs out there, especially in the Arduino community. It is often utilised to drive multiple LEDs with serial input. A project of mine used it drive multiple LEDs. I found that the LED brightness reduces when too many of them are on. This made me wonder how much current can this IC actually supply. I measured the IV curve of the output of the 74HC595 last year. But I found that the measurement was kind of flawed. Therefore, I measured it again in this blog and documented the details in my setup.

The test setup.

Last time, I only pulled current from 1 output on the 74HC595. However, the 74HC595 typically drives multiple LEDs when it used as an LED driver. Therefore, I pulled current from all 8 outputs of the 74HC595 this time.

I connected all 8 outputs together to the drain of a MOSFET, which will act as a constant current sink. The gate voltage of the MOSFET was controlled by an STM32's DAC. To prevent the MOSFET from overheating, the gate voltage was given in 1ms-pulses.

The total current provided by all 8 outputs were passed through a 2.2Ω resistor. The voltage across the resistor was then amplified by a current sense amplifier with a gain of 15 V/V. The current sense circuitry map 0 to 100mA to 0 to 3.3V at the STM32's ADC input.

We can divide the measured current by 8 to find the current of 1 output of the 74HC595.

The current sense amplifier.

I used the Holtek HT92632 precision opamp in the current sense amplifier. It can be powered with 5V and has a typical input offset voltage of 15 uV. The inputs and outputs of this opamp can swing to the supply rail, which is crucial for this application. It has low bandwidth and slew rate, but we are only doing low speed measurements so it is acceptable. Another reason for choosing this opamp is its availability. Holtek has online store which make purchasing their chips very easy (at least in Taiwan).

This is where I screwed up last time. I made sure te Kelvin connection is correct this time.

The measured current was first passed through a 2.2Ω resistor in Kelvin connection. Then, I setup a differential amplifier of gain 15 V/V with the HT92632 opamp. With these two stages, the combined trans-impedance gain is 33Ω.

Before using the current sense amplifier, I decided to measure its DC transfer function first. I used a known-accurate current source (from a DC power supply) to pass current through the 2.2Ω resistor and measured the output voltage.

Measuring the offset voltage of the setup.

To measure the offset voltage caused by the opamp and the breadboard contact, I setup an amplifier with a gain of approximately 10000. I shorted the input of the amplifier together to 2.5V and measured the output of the amplifier (relative to 2.5V).

The input offset of the first stage was amplified by 10000 times, while the input offset of the second stage was amplified by 100 times. The second term can be neglected since the first term is approximately 100 times larger. Therefore, by dividing the output voltage of the amplifier by 10000, we can calculate the input offset of the setup.

The total input offset implies the uncertainty in the input voltage. Since we are using a 2.2Ω resistor, the uncertainty in current associated with the input offset voltage would be 52 uV * 2.2Ω = 0.11 mA.

Controlling the test current.

I was using the 2N7000 N-channel MOSFET. The first thing to do was to verify the Id-Vg characteristic. The 74HC595 datasheet states the absolute maximum current is 70mA, so I wanted to verify that the 2N7000 could sink this much current with the gate voltage that STM32 can provide. Due to the current sense resistor between the ground and the source of the MOSFET, I expected the Id-Vg characteristic to slightly degrade.

The 2N7000 doesn't have a heat sink mounted, which meant that we cannot apply current and voltage on the device at the same time. This is acceptable in normal switching applications since either voltage or current will be 0 when the device is on or off, respectively. However, I was operating this MOSFET in the saturation region, so both voltage and current would stress the device at the same time. This would lead to heat generation faster than the package could dissipate. If we didn't take care, the MOSFET might blow up.

To prevent the MOSFET from overheating, it was only turned on in short pulse. In a measurement cycle, the following happens.

  1. The gate voltage of the MOSFET is driven to the desired level by the DAC on the STM32.
  2. Wait for 1ms for the voltage and current to stabilise.
  3. Measure some samples with the ADC.
  4. Set the gate voltage to 0V to cut off the current.
  5. Send the measured data back to the computer. The communication takes a while, and the MOSFET can cool down during this period.

When testing the Id-Vg characteristic, I applied 4V from drain to ground to ensure the MOSFET is saturated.

Actually measuring the 74HC595.

It is finally time to measure the IV curve of the 74HC595. I connected all 8 outputs together with diodes to the MOSFET current sink. The voltage of one output was also measured by the STM32 through a resistor divider. The serial input of the 74HC595 was tied to logic 1, since we wanted to measure the current sourcing capability. The clocks were connected to a GPIO on the STM32.

First, the STM32 sends 8 clock pulses to fill the shift register with ones. The 9th clock pulse is used to update the storage registers in the 74HC595, which in turn drive the outputs. These two actions ensures all 8 outputs will be at logic high.

Then, the STM32 starts to pulse the gate voltage of the MOSFET until the DAC of the STM32 reaches 3.3V or the measured current reaches 70mA. During each pulse, the voltage and current of the 74HC595 output are recorded. A Python script on my computer receives the measured data and makes the IV curve plot.

The output IV curve of the 74HC595 exhibits a linear relation. The initial drop to 4.95V is due to the voltage drop across the wires that connects the power supply to the breadboard. Then, the 74HC595 has an output resistance of 31Ω and the output voltage drops linearly with the current.

I also decided to do the same measurement as last time for comparison. That is, drawing current from only one output on the 74HC595.

Initially, the output voltage also drops linearly with an output resistance of around 30Ω. Then, the voltage starts to drop sharply after drawing 50mA of current.
